# The Perceptron: Motivation & Theory

## Three Fundamental Questions

To understand how organisms learn and think, we must first answer three questions:

1. **How is information sensed?** How does a biological system detect the physical world?
2. **How is information stored?** What form does memory take?
3. **How does stored information influence behavior?** How do we use what we remember?

The first question—sensory physiology—has been well studied. But the second and third remain deeply mysterious and highly speculative.

## Two Theories of Memory

### The Coded Memory Hypothesis

Some theorists propose that sensory information is stored as **coded representations or images**—a one-to-one mapping between stimulus and stored pattern. This hypothesis is appealingly simple: if you understood the nervous system's "wiring diagram," you could reconstruct memories like developing a photographic negative. Many formal brain models are built on this idea.

### The Connectionist Hypothesis

An alternative tradition, stemming from British empiricism, suggests a different mechanism: **images may never be recorded at all**. Instead, the nervous system acts as an intricate switching network where retention takes the form of **new connections, or pathways, between centers of activity**. Information is stored not as a topographic representation but as a preference for particular responses—**the information is contained in associations rather than representations**.

## Why the Perceptron

The perceptron theory takes the **connectionist position**. It was developed for a hypothetical nervous system designed to illustrate fundamental properties of intelligent systems without becoming too entangled in the unknown specifics of actual biological organisms.

Rosenblatt's approach differs from purely logical models (which rely on symbolic logic and Boolean algebra) by using **probability theory** instead. This enables analysis of systems where only the gross organization can be characterized, and the precise structure remains unknown—much like the actual brain.

The key insight: a randomly connected network, subject to proper learning rules, can learn to recognize patterns, generalize from experience, and form associations—without any "master blueprint" encoding the final solution.
